Kabupaten Bengkalis holds one of the most extensive peatland complexes
on Sumatra's eastern coast — over 1.1 million hectares spanning 11
sub-districts, from the island communities of Rupat in the north to the
oil palm–dominated interior of Mandau in the south. Between 2015 and 2024,
67,619 satellite fire detections were recorded within its boundaries.

The question at the centre of this analysis is not simply *when* fires
burn — it is *why the pattern is so different across sub-districts that
share the same regional climate signal*, and what that asymmetry implies
for early warning design.

## ENSO vs. hotspot count, 2015–2024

The dual-axis chart below plots the monthly Oceanic Niño Index (ONI) against

(ONI peak +2.6) produced the largest single-month fire event on record; the

that had depleted peat water tables — produced the second highest.

The 2022–2023 La Niña suppression is equally diagnostic. Three consecutive
La Niña years brought above-average rainfall and hotspot counts fell to their
lowest since before 2015. The pattern confirms ENSO as the dominant climate
driver — but with two critical exceptions: the 2019 antecedent-moisture
anomaly, and the persistent non-response in Mandau.

## Predictor hierarchy: climate meets land-use change

One of the most significant findings from the GEE non-climatic factor
analysis is that **deforestation rate** (r = −0.299, p < 0.001) entered the
global top-3 predictor ranking, ahead of ONI (r = +0.235). Population
density followed closely at rank 4. The chart below shows the full

The negative sign on deforestation requires careful interpretation. It does
not mean that more deforestation suppresses fire — it reflects that
sub-districts with high deforestation rates (Rupat Utara at 38.5%, Bantan at
34.0%) tend to have lower *peat-specific* hotspot counts because their fires
are surface-level agricultural burns not always detected by MODIS as
high-confidence thermal anomalies. The structural relationship is amplifying,
not protective.

## Lag-decay: different sub-districts, different warning windows

Perhaps the most operationally significant finding is the variability in
**ENSO lag-time** across sub-districts. The chart below shows the
Spearman correlation between ONI and hotspot count at lags 0–3 months
for each sub-district (columns = Lag 0, 1, 2, 3).

The practical implication is stark: **a single uniform warning system
cannot serve Bengkalis efficiently.**

- **Rupat Utara** peaks at Lag 0 — it needs *immediate* alert protocols
  the moment ONI crosses the El Niño threshold.
- **Bandar Laksamana** and **Siak Kecil** peak at Lag 2 — a 2-month
  lead time aligns with NOAA's seasonal ENSO forecast horizon, giving
  agencies a meaningful intervention window.
- **Bathin Solapan** and **Talang Muandau** peak at Lag 5–6 months,
  reflecting deep peat (> 4 m) that requires prolonged drought before
  the water table drops to critical depth.
- **Mandau** shows no significant correlation at any lag — EWS
  protocols based on ENSO are structurally inapplicable here.

## The XGBoost model and SHAP attribution

The final predictive model (XGBoost, 5-fold stratified CV, 1,298 monthly
samples) achieved **CV-AUC = 0.8181 ± 0.016**, meaningfully outperforming
the Random Forest baseline (0.8179 ± 0.026) — with critically lower variance,
which matters for operational reliability.

SHAP attribution via TreeExplainer confirmed the following driver hierarchy:

| Rank | Feature | SHAP direction |
|------|---------|---------------|
| 1 | SPEI-3 Hargreaves-Samani | Low (dry) → fire ↑ |
| 2 | Rainfall (CHIRPS) | Low → fire ↑ |
| 3 | Deforestation rate | Modulates spatially |
| 4 | ONI (ENSO) | Positive → fire ↑ |
| 5 | Water Occurrence (JRC) | High → fire ↓ (protective) |

SPEI-3 ranked above SPI-3 in SHAP importance — consistent with the
theoretical expectation that temperature effects on evapotranspiration
become increasingly critical during El Niño years, which tend to be warmer
than neutral-phase years. The Hargreaves-Samani formulation captures this
without requiring radiation data, making it tractable for operational use with
ERA5-Land inputs.

## The Mandau anomaly: when anthropogenic fire decouples from climate

Four independent lines of evidence converge on the same diagnosis for Mandau:

**1. Near-zero ONI correlation** across all lags (r = −0.020 at Lag 0,
never exceeding 0.055 at Lag 5). No sub-district in a 10-year ENSO
teleconnection study should show this pattern unless the fire ignition
mechanism is structurally independent of rainfall.

**2. Inverted El Niño/La Niña fire ratio** of 0.73×. Mandau is the only
sub-district where P(fire | La Niña) > P(fire | El Niño). The proposed
mechanism: during La Niña, when rainfall is above average, oil palm
farmers exploit brief dry windows for controlled land-clearing burns.
During extreme El Niño, when conditions are genuinely dangerous,
uncontrolled burns become too risky — and plantation managers halt burning
to avoid liability.

**3. Highest oil palm fraction** in the regency (16.9% of land area) and the
**highest population density** (2.56 persons km⁻²). Both indicators of
intensive land use with regular ignition activity tied to agricultural cycles.

**4. Lowest total hotspot count** (n = 29 over 10 years) despite being one
of the more economically active sub-districts. MODIS tends to undercount
agricultural burns that lack the spatial clustering signature of peat fires,
particularly when DBSCAN filtering removes isolated industrial false positives
from the Duri gas flare complex.

The implication for BPBD Bengkalis: **Mandau requires a fundamentally
different monitoring framework** — satellite-based land-clearing detection
(Landsat change detection, Sentinel-2 near-real-time), zero-burn regulation
enforcement, and alternative livelihood programs, not an ENSO-based alert
threshold.

## Methodology

**Hotspot data:** MODIS Active Fire Product (Collection 6.1), 2015–2024.
DBSCAN spatial clustering applied to remove Duri industrial false positives:
anchor threshold ≥ 70% confidence; rescue radius 1.5 km for 50–69% confidence
points. Final dataset: 51,086 hotspots from 67,619 raw (75.5% retention).

**Climate data:** CHIRPS v2.0 (~5.5 km, monthly precipitation) + ERA5-Land
(~9 km, temperature) extracted via Google Earth Engine at the centroid of each
sub-district. Replaces NASA POWER (~55 km) used in earlier notebook versions.

**Drought index:** SPEI-3 Hargreaves-Samani. PET estimated as
`PET_HS = 0.0023 × Rₐ × (Tmean + 17.8) × √(Tmax − Tmin)`, requiring only
Tmin, Tmax, and latitude (all available from ERA5-Land). Water balance
(P − PET) aggregated over 3-month windows and standardised using the
log-logistic (Fisk) distribution.

**Non-climate factors:** Six GEE layers — oil palm fraction (ESA WorldCover),
deforestation 2015–2024 (Hansen GFC v1.11), water occurrence (JRC GSW v1.4),
population density (WorldPop 2020), fire-prone land cover, NDVI trend
(Landsat 8 Collection 2 SR).

**Machine learning:** XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016), 13 features, 5-fold
stratified CV on 1,298 monthly samples (28.4% fire months). SHAP attribution
via TreeExplainer (Lundberg & Lee, 2017).

**Spatial autocorrelation:** Moran's I tested with queen contiguity,
row-standardised weights, 999 Monte Carlo permutations (libpysal).
